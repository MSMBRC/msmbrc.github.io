A neo-medieval village built almost entirely between 1905 and 1916 at the behest of count **Giuseppe Visconti di Modrone**, who wanted to create an ideal settlement inspired by medieval aesthetics, designed together with architect **Alfredo Campanini**. It is technically a "historical fake", but conceived down to the smallest detail: not just a backdrop, but a living village complete with homes, artisan workshops, trade schools, a theatre, and a nursery school. The underlying idea was to revive craftsmanship as a form of human creativity, in contrast with the industrial logic of the time. The village wraps in a horseshoe shape around the medieval castle, the only genuinely ancient structure on site.

## What to see

The **Castle** (1395) is the historical heart of the village: a quadrangular layout with 4 towers, open for guided visits on weekends and public holidays. Inside you'll find the banquet hall, the library, the armoury, and the private quarters. Surrounding it is the **Park**, covering around 150,000 sqm, with trees from all over the world, a maze, an Italian-style garden, and a scenic viewpoint. It also includes the "Butterfly Heaven" and a dedicated owl watching area.

**Piazza del Biscione** is the heart of the village: the Palazzo dell'Istituzione, the Albergo del Biscione, a fountain, and a well in terracotta and pink marble all face onto it. Nearby, the **Cortevecchia** hosts a free permanent museum of early 20th-century farming tools and the well-known statue of Aloisa.

The **Church of SS. Cosma e Damiano** is the oldest building, with its original structure dating back to the 13th century and rebuilt in 1650. It houses frescoes attributed to the school of Giotto and the Chapel of the Madonna of Lourdes with votive offerings. The **Palazzo dell'Istituzione** is home to the Wax Museum — dedicated to key figures in the history of Piacenza — and the Museum of Torture, with explanatory panels and an Inquisition-themed escape room. The artisan shops are open year-round.

## The legend of Aloisa

**Aloisa** is the village's mythical figure: the daughter of a castle lord, she died of grief after being betrayed by her husband. Legend has it that her ghost still wanders the walls. Her statue stands in the Cortevecchia, and by tradition visitors — especially couples — leave notes and small gifts asking for her protection.

## Practical info

- The village is **always open** and does not close in the evening (individual shops and attractions have their own hours)
- **Paid parking** at the north and south entrances
- Dining options available inside the village (restaurants and street food); picnics are also welcome
- Castle + park visit: ~€23 adults / €13 reduced

## History

- **~1000 AD** — earliest documented traces of the area (land donated to the monastery of San Savino in Piacenza)
- **1395** — the castle is built at the behest of Gian Galeazzo Visconti as a wedding gift for his sister Beatrice, who married the Piacenza nobleman Giovanni Anguissola
- **1521** — the village is razed to the ground by French troops under Lautrec
- **1547** — the assassination of duke Pier Luigi Farnese is plotted within the castle walls
- **1870** — the last Anguissola heir dies without descendants; the castle passes to the Visconti family
- **Early 1900s** — Giuseppe Visconti di Modrone restores the ruined castle and builds the neo-medieval village
- **1937** — King Vittorio Emanuele III grants Giuseppe the title of Duke of Grazzano Visconti
- **1986** — the Emilia-Romagna region awards it the title of City of Art
